Food of Istanbul

Istanbul is a city where food reflects its rich history and cultural diversity. Sitting between Europe and Asia, Istanbul’s cuisine blends Ottoman, Middle Eastern, Mediterranean, and Balkan influences, offering a unique and unforgettable experience for visitors.

Street food is everywhere — from simit (sesame bread rings) and roasted chestnuts to döner kebabs and balık ekmek (fish sandwiches) by the Galata Bridge. Walking through bustling markets like the Grand Bazaar or Spice Bazaar, you’ll find Turkish delight, dried fruits, spices, olives, and cheeses, filling the air with delicious aromas.

For a sit-down meal, traditional meyhanes serve meze platters with small dishes like hummus, stuffed grape leaves, and grilled seafood, usually enjoyed with rakı, Turkey’s famous anise-flavored drink. Istanbul is also known for its desserts, especially baklava, made of layered filo pastry with nuts and syrup, and künefe, a hot cheese-filled sweet topped with crispy shredded wheat.

Tea and Turkish coffee are central to daily life, served with or without sweets. Today, Istanbul’s food scene continues to evolve, with trendy cafes and gourmet restaurants joining traditional eateries. Whether simple street snacks or elaborate meals, food in Istanbul offers a taste of the city’s soul and centuries of culinary tradition.

Simit

Sesame-crusted bread rings, a popular street snack often eaten with tea or cheese.

Döner Kebab

Spit-roasted seasoned meat served in wraps, plates, or sandwiches across the city.

Baklava

Rich dessert of filo layers, pistachios or walnuts, and sweet syrup or honey.

Turkish coffee

Turkish coffee is a rich, unfiltered brew served in small cups, known for its strong flavor and cultural ritual.
